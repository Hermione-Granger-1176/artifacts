Concepts explained

A token is a chunk of text, somewhere between a character and a word. "unhappiness" might be split into "un", "happiness". "cat" stays as one token. Spaces and punctuation are tokens too.

The model never sees letters. It sees a stream of token IDs, each one a number pointing to an entry in its vocabulary (typically 50,000 to 100,000 tokens). Every response you get is the model picking the next token, one at a time, until it decides to stop.

Rule of thumb: 1 token ≈ 0.75 words in English

Before sampling, the model assigns a raw score (logit) to every possible next token. Temperature divides all those scores before converting them into probabilities.

Low temperature (e.g. 0.2) sharpens the distribution, making the top token dominate. High temperature (e.g. 2.0) flattens it, spreading probability more evenly and making surprising tokens viable.

Think of it like a group decision: low temperature is a confident leader who always picks the obvious answer. High temperature is a brainstorm where even the weird ideas get a vote.

Code and facts: 0.2 to 0.5. Creative writing: 0.8 to 1.2. Chaos: above 1.5

Top P sets a cumulative probability threshold. The model ranks all tokens by probability, then keeps adding them to the candidate pool until the running total hits P. Everything below the cutoff is excluded.

At P=0.9, if the top two tokens already account for 90% of the probability mass, only those two are sampled from. At P=1.0, every token is in play.

This adapts to context. When the model is confident, the nucleus is small. When it is uncertain, more tokens pass the threshold naturally, giving variety where it makes sense.

0.7 to 0.95 is the typical sweet spot in production
