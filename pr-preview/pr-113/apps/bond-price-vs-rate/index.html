Why it happens

The payments are locked in. The price is not.

When this bond was issued it promised a fixed coupon: the same dollar payment every year until it matures. That promise never changes. So the only lever the market has to make an older bond compete with newly issued ones is its price. Raise the coupon in the control panel above and watch how it changes whether buyers pay up or demand a discount.

Premium, par, discount
If your coupon beats today's market rate, buyers pay more than the $1,000 face value (a premium). If it lags, they only buy at a discount. If the two match, the bond trades right at par.
The mathematics

One formula prices every bond.

A dollar arriving in t years is worth only 1/(1 + r)t dollars today, because today's dollar could be invested at the market rate r and grow to match it. Every future coupon and the face repayment gets shrunk by that factor, and the price is simply their sum.

P = n ∑ t = 1 C (1 + r)t + F (1 + r)n
  • C annual coupon = $50
  • r market rate = 5.0%
  • n years to maturity = 10
  • F face value = $1,000

How hard it swings

The longer the bond, the bigger the move.

A rate change is applied to every remaining payment, and long bonds have far more payments still waiting to be discounted. So the same rate move barely dents a short bond but can hammer a long one. That sensitivity is what professionals call duration. Drag the years slider in the control panel above to move your bond along the chart.

The missing piece

There isn't one interest rate. There's a curve.

Everything above priced the bond against a single market rate. In reality the market quotes a different yield for every maturity, and the line through them is the yield curve, one of the most watched signals in finance. Pick a shape to see what the market pays at each maturity.

The ripple

Why one rate move touches the whole economy.

Existing bondholders

Everyone already holding older, lower-coupon bonds sees their market value fall. This is why a "safe" bond fund can post losses when central banks hike rates, even though no borrower has missed a payment.

Tomorrow's bonds

Brand-new bonds get issued at the new, higher coupons. Fresh buyers get a better deal, which is the market repricing inflation and risk expectations in real time.

The real economy

Central banks move rates to steer inflation and growth. Those yields ripple into mortgages, corporate borrowing, and government debt costs, tightening or loosening spending everywhere.
